# CTA/FDA Roundtable: Wearables & FDA's General Wellness Guidance

February 13, 2026

FDA Guidance here

On February 13, 2026, FDA leadership met with Consumer Technology Association (CTA) staff and member companies to discuss FDA's recent guidance differentiating wellness products under FDA enforcement discretion and FDA-regulated medical devices. After answering clarifying questions on the new guidance, roundtable participants discussed opportunities for FDA and CTA to collaborate moving forward. 

This summary of the CTA/FDA Roundtable is organized as follows: 

- Key takeaways from FDA leadership and CTA Members
- Next Steps and Action Items for CTA
- Discussion Summary
- Attendee List 

## Key Takeaways

FDA leadership emphasized these points:

- The wellness guidance is intended to create a balance between empowering consumers and safety concerns. FDA hopes the guidance increases consumer access to wellness products while also ensuring these products do not exacerbate user anxiety or overwhelm the health care system. 
- Wellness products do not diagnose. Wellness products cannot diagnose (e.g., stating that a user has hypertension or tachycardia, or use clinical thresholds like 130/80 mmHg for blood pressure) or they will be considered a medical device under FDA's regulatory purview. 
- Industry should take the lead in the non-FDA-regulated wellness space. FDA is limited in the resources it can offer because it does not regulate wellness products. Industry stakeholders are encouraged to develop standards and offer best practices in the wellness arena. 
- TEMPO Program. FDA highlighted the Technology-Enabled Meaningful Patient Outcomes (TEMPO) program as a pathway for wellness products to transition to medical device status. Applications being reviewed; FDA will reach out to eligible participants after March 2, 2026.

CTA members’ viewpoints included:

- Appreciation for the updated guidance. CTA members view the guidance as a meaningful step forward in clarifying the line between medical devices and wellness products.
- Concerns about product validation, appropriate communications with consumers, and inclusion of wellness data in EHRs. CTA members requested clarification about what "validated" means in the context of wellness products, how companies could communicate health metrics to consumers without making medical diagnoses, and whether confusion and liability risk would result from wellness product data being included alongside validated medical device data in electronic health records (EHRs).

## Next Steps / Action Items for CTA

1. Industry-Led Standards Development. CTA will consider developing industry guidance on "what good looks like" for wellness product validation.
    - FDA offered to provide existing medical device standards as reference material
    - FDA willing to review industry-developed guidance drafts, though this is lower priority given wellness products are not regulated
2. Communication and Education. CTA will explore collaborative approaches to do outreach about wellness products to manufacturers, providers, and consumers. There is a need to spread awareness on the new guidance and help stakeholders discern the difference between clinically-validated and non-clinically validated data. 
    - FDA webinar Q&A from the day prior will be made publicly available
    - FDA may develop FAQ webpage specific to this guidance (similar to Mobile Medical Apps guidance)
    - FDA suggested that there are potential partnerships with professional societies (DiMe mentioned as already active in wearables education)
3. Ongoing Dialogue. CTA will consider hosting a listening session to discuss EHR integration issues with healthcare organizations.
    - FDA expressed interest in additional conversations to understand industry challenges and boundary cases
    - FDA encouraged pre-submission consultations for products near the wellness/medical device boundary

## DISCUSSION SUMMARY 

Several CTA members expressed appreciation for the new guidance but wanted to ask clarifying questions. Specific areas in the guidance were explored, and suggestions were made about how stakeholders like CTA could help offer standards and best practices. 

Issues discussed:

Validation – For certain products that may be considered general wellness products, FDA's guidance says it may not include values that mimic those used clinically unless validated. CTA members urged FDA to consider that because the guidance does not specify what “validation” should look like, some manufacturers may mislead consumers saying their products are “validated” using its own definition that does not imply a rigorous process including evidence and evaluation. 
- FDA said that the guidance document does not define or specify validation, as it does not regulate those products, but recommended CTA members leverage existing standards and processes. FDA will only independently review products if there is a public health risk. 
- Manufacturers are not required to publicly publish their validation evidence, but FDA encourages them to do so to build consumer trust. If evidence is not published, FDA may ask to review a summary from the manufacturer if they are making a claim that prompts review. 

User notification features – FDA’s guidance says that general wellness products may include notification features informing users to seek care by a medical provider, as long as it does not include information that would be considered a diagnosis (e.g., clinical thresholds, specifying a condition or disease, characterizing the output as abnormal or diagnostic) or to help users manage an ongoing chronic disease. 
- FDA emphasized that if a product includes a notification that would be considered a “diagnosis” it would be a medical device. Including clinical guidelines next to a notification / metric would be considered a diagnosis. 
- One CTA member asked if a notification informing users that their “scores are persistently high” or if a user is “outside the wellness range” would be permissible. FDA said yes, however, the product could not necessarily say the user crossed a specified threshold by X amount because that would be a diagnosis. 
- Unresolved: FDA did not have a clear answer on whether a wellness product that allows a user to set their own thresholds to be alerted for given metrics would qualify the product as a medical device. 

Determining “clinical values” - FDA clarified that manufacturers should use their best judgement to determine whether a value is used clinically. Dr. Tarver suggested a good place to begin is considering if there are clinical guidelines, codes, existing FDA-authorized devices that provides analogous measurements in a clinical context, or literature that speaks to a value, the starting presumption would be that is a clinical value. Dr. Tarver emphasized that with the presence of these things, people will likely infer a value is clinical. 

Data quality – One CTA member warned that health systems may not know how to discern FDA-approved medical-grade device data and non-clinically validated data from a general wellness product when integrating the data into the EMR. 
- FDA said that this is an issue within the health system (i.e., what they want to integrate into their record and what they want to accept in terms of liability), outside the bounds of FDA’s regulatory purview. FDA suggested this conversation may be better suited for ASTP/ONC, hospital associations, and/or specialty provider groups. 

Reporting concerns and less than ideal outcomes – FDA encouraged CTA members to report concerns related to products potentially marketed as wellness products but are seeming to make claims that would qualify it as a medical device. FDA also has an annual report on the safety of non-device functions. 
- Generally, FDA encouraged CTA members to report concerns for any specific products or devices to CDRH’s device allegations inbox.
