Summary
Country gating for assessment launch was simplified, data processing for location tracking required development, and assessment generation timing optimization was debated.Country Gating Simplification DecisionThe team simplified the country gating approach: if a user uploads any data from an eligible country during an assessment cycle, an assessment will be produced, given minimum day requirements are met. The group decided that if a user never resides in an eligible country, no assessment will be produced.Location Tracking Implementation NeedsDue to the need to track data origin for country gating, a location system must be built into Ingest to track data origin using Geoip. The team expressed a preference against storing a time series of location data due to potential legal hurdles.Assessment Timing and ScalingScalability concerns were raised regarding computing all monthly assessment results on the 1st of the month due to load and cost. Pramod Rudrapatna preferred normalizing the assessment date to the 1st of every month for a simpler user message.

Details
Country Gating for Assessment Launch: The team discussed country gating, noting the Business Requirements Document (BRD) suggests launching in 50-plus countries, similar to Whoop's approach. Pramod Rudrapatna confirmed that country gating will be required, and a regulatory assessment is currently underway (00:00:00). The consensus was reached to simplify the country gating approach: if a user uploads any data from an eligible country during an assessment cycle, an assessment will be produced at the end of the month, regardless of where the rest of the data originated (00:06:40) (00:09:24).
Handling User Travel and Data Collection: Pramod Rudrapatna outlined three scenarios for users who travel: they will receive an assessment if they meet the minimum required days (7 or 14) regardless of travel; they will receive an assessment if all countries visited during the assessment period are supported and the minimum days are met; they will not receive an assessment if they travel to an unsupported country and have not met the minimum duration (00:01:00). It was clarified that the waveform foundation model should run irrespective of the country, and the three scenarios apply only to the classification and assessment part (00:03:04). The group decided that if a user never resides in an eligible country, no assessment will be produced (00:12:22).
Data Processing and Location Tracking for Gating: someone in Burger And Fries (us-sfo-fre215) noted that the classification model runs once per month, and they do not currently attach country data to the processed data, which makes it challenging to determine the origin of the 14 days of collected data (00:03:04). To support the country gating decision, someone in Burger And Fries (us-sfo-fre215) indicated they will need to build some sort of location system into Ingest to track data origin, acknowledging this will be based on physical location (Geoip) rather than language locale (00:09:24). They also expressed a preference against storing a time series of location data due to potential legal hurdles (00:10:22).
User Onboarding and Feature Access: Heena Sethi inquired about the process, given that there is no onboarding or explicit consent required for the feature. Pramod Rudrapatna confirmed that users will need to confirm inputs, such as height for the blood pressure model or demography for insulin resistance, via an education screen, but this is not considered consent (00:03:56). The feature will be turned on via a full-screen takeover when it launches, and the assessment will run regardless of whether users confirm their demographic inputs (00:05:02).
Tile Availability and Consistency for Users: Cynthia Cruz proposed gating the tile visibility, especially for users who move to an unsupported location (00:08:29). The team decided that if a user has no history of a conclusive assessment and is not in an eligible country, the tile would not be shown (00:12:22). Someone in Burger And Fries (us-sfo-fre215) added that if a user has an assessment history, the tile will remain visible, similar to the approach used for IRN (00:08:29).
Opt-Out Mechanism and Notification Settings: The discussion moved to opt-out functionality, with Cynthia Cruz suggesting following the pattern used for HRN, which requires building a dedicated settings page (00:14:13). Pramod Rudrapatna questioned the necessity of an opt-out feature since the assessment is a glanceable view and not solely a notification, suggesting leaning on General Data Deletion (GDD) instead (00:15:13). The team agreed that push notifications for positive results would be necessary, and Pramod Rudrapatna and Cynthia Cruz will consult with legal and revisit the decision to avoid overcomplicating the product (00:17:00).
Deletion of Assessments: someone in Burger And Fries (us-sfo-fre215) inquired about the lack of a "delete assessment" button in the UI (00:18:03). Cynthia Cruz and Pramod Rudrapatna agreed that they should rely on GDD for data deletion to avoid unnecessarily adding more elements to the user experience (00:19:00).
Timing and Scheduling of Monthly Assessments: someone in Burger And Fries (us-sfo-fre215) raised concerns about the scalability and risk of computing results for all users on the first day of the month. They proposed starting an individual user's monthly assessment on the day they onboard, with the results computed a month later, instead of aligning everyone to the first of the month (00:19:44). Pramod Rudrapatna preferred normalizing the assessment date to the first of every month for a simpler user message, noting that the assessment could still run even if the last day of data is missing, provided the minimum 14 days have been met (00:22:22) (00:24:22).
Addressing Load and Feature Consistency: Ryan emphasized that the monthly triggering mechanism needs to be solved collectively across all features that have a monthly requirement, such as insulin resistance and sleep metrics, to manage the load efficiently (00:23:32) (00:26:50). Someone in Burger And Fries (us-sfo-fre215) explained the scalability concern stems from needing to provision resources for the massive load on one day, which would sit idle for the rest of the month, increasing cost (00:30:18). Ryan and Pramod Rudrapatna discussed the possibility of using foundational models to lighten the monthly load and suggested aligning the assessment generation between the morning and evening, showing it at an evening brief moment (00:28:40) (00:32:12).
Optimization and Handling Late Data: someone in Burger And Fries (us-sfo-fre215) asked about the possibility of early mean aggregation of embeddings to mitigate scalability concerns (00:33:00). The team noted that they would explore sequencing the current naive aggregation method with a more robust one as a quick follow-up (00:33:49). To address late-arriving data, it was determined that they would proceed with the assessment using the data already collected, and if data arrives too late, it would not be processed, though this could be problematic for regulated features. The team also discussed using tentative, in-progress, and completed states for assessments, similar to the apnea approach, allowing for pre-computation and better handling of data syncs (00:34:44) (00:38:47).
