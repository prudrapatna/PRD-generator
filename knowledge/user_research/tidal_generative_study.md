# User Research: Tidal Generative Study

**Date:** October 7, 2025
**Owner:** Jeremy Schwark (jeremyschwark@)
**Method:** Unmoderated Survey (n=328, Android users)
**Audience:**
*   **Sample Size:** 328 Android users (External).
*   **Condition Status:** Includes Pre-existing conditions (Hypertension, High Cholesterol, Asthma, Tachycardia, Pre-diabetes).
*   **Top User Goals:** Manage health condition, Lose weight, Monitor overall health.

---

## 1. User Segmentation & Current Habits

### Knowledge & Familiarity
*   **BP Awareness:** Most users are somewhat or very familiar with blood pressure concepts.
*   **Confidence:** High confidence in knowing their own typical BP numbers (likely due to frequent measurement).
*   **Hypertension Awareness:**
    *   Most participants correctly identified common hypertension symptoms.
    *   Many could estimate Stage 1 hypertension cutoffs.
    *   **Perceived Risk:** Most rated their personal risk as "Moderate."

### Measurement Behavior
*   **Prevalence:** >75% of participants have measured their own BP.
    *   **Chronic Strugglers:** 86% measure their own BP.
    *   **Proactive Adopters / Anxious Evaluators:** 75% measure their own BP.
*   **Methods:** Automatic upper arm monitors (most common) and wearables.
*   **Ownership:** Most who measure own a standalone BP monitor (especially Chronic Strugglers & Proactive Adopters).
*   **Frequency:** >50% of those who measure do so at least **weekly**.
*   **Action Taken:** Most self-monitor; some share with HCP; a few report **adjusting medications on their own** (Risk!).

### Wearable Usage
*   **Adoption:** High familiarity with wearables.
*   **Current Monitoring:** Many claim to monitor conditions (BP, SpO2) that their device likely *cannot* do, suggesting a placebo effect or confusion with other metrics (Heart Rate).

## 2. Feature Concept Testing

**Concept:** "A new feature that passively checks for signs of high blood pressure while youâ€™re wearing the device. It would send you an alert if it detected high blood pressure, which could be a sign that you have hypertension."

### Appeal & Interest
*   **Likelihood to Enable:** **94% Total** (65% Very Likely, 29% Somewhat Likely).
*   **Utility:** **93%** felt it would be somewhat/very useful.
*   **Recommendation Score:** >80% would recommend it to a friend/family member.
*   **Purchase Influence:** Majority said this feature would **increase likelihood** of buying a wearable (for self or family).

### Why People Want It (Pros)
1.  **Effortless / Automatic:** "Set it and forget it." Removes burden of manual checks.
2.  **Proactive / Early Detection:** Catching the "Silent Killer" before symptoms appear.
3.  **Safety Net:** "It can save my life." Persistent sense of safety.
4.  **Management:** For those with known risks, it's a tool for ongoing management.

### Why People Worry (Cons)
1.  **Accuracy / Reliability:** Skepticism that a watch matches a cuff. "It's never right." Fear of false warnings.
2.  **Alert Anxiety:** Fear that frequent/false alerts will cause stress (spiking BP further). "Just wearing it would raise my BP."
3.  **Data Privacy:** Fear of data sharing with advertisers/companies. "Constant recording of my private health."

## 3. Functionality Preferences & Expectations

### Important Aspects (Top 3)
1.  **Accuracy:** "I want it to accurately do what it says it will (detect hypertension)."
2.  **Actionability:** "I want to understand what the feature is telling me and what to do about it."
3.  **HCP Sharing:** "I want to share data with an HCP."

### Next Steps Desired
*   **Top Requests:**
    *   Share data with HCP directly from app.
    *   Follow-up check-ins/insights.
    *   Info on lifestyle changes to reduce BP.

### Notification Dynamics
*   **Reminder Frequency:**
    *   **Unviewed Result:** Users want **Daily Reminders**.
    *   **Dismissed (But Still High):** Users want **Daily Re-alerts**.
*   **Action Threshold:**
    *   **76%** would need **multiple notifications** before calling a doctor.
    *   Many would interpret 1 notification = 1 high reading (not a persistent state).
*   **Response to "Ignored" Alerts:**
    *   If alerts continue, most would:
        1.  Take a manual BP measurement (Validation).
        2.  Follow up with HCP.
        3.  Wait for symptoms (Minority).
*   **Emergency Response:** Most would **not** call 911, but a small subset might consider it.

### False Alarm Tolerance
*   **Sensitivity:** Extremely High.
*   **Churn Risk:** Over **50%** would consider turning off the feature after receiving just **1-2 false alarms in a year**.

---

## 4. Strategic Implications (Key Takeaways)

1.  **Misconception Management:** Users will likely think this is a *continuous* monitor (real-time). We must clarify it is a *passive spot-checker* to manage expectations.
2.  **The "Validation" Loop:** Users *will* validate with a home cuff immediately. The app should lean into this behavior rather than ignore it.
    *   *Risk:* Users adjusting meds based on watch data alone.
3.  **The "Boy Who Cried Wolf":** High sensitivity to false alarms means we must prioritize **Precision over Recall**.
4.  **Messaging Urgency:** Users may ignore a single notification. Messaging must clarify that **One Alert = Multiple Detections**, warranting immediate attention.
5.  **HCP Integration:** The ability to share/export results is a critical feature, not a "nice-to-have."
6.  **Onboarding:** Must proactively address Accuracy, Anxiety, and Privacy to convert the "skeptics."
